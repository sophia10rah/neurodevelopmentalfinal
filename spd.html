Sensory Processing Disorder (SPD)

Key Facts & Research

Core Features of SPD

Definition

A neurological condition where the brain struggles to process and respond to sensory input (sound, touch, taste, movement).

Prevalence

  • 5–16% of children
  • Often co-occurs with ADHD, autism, or anxiety

Diagnosis

Not in the DSM-5 but recognized by occupational therapists (OTs).

Brain Mechanisms

Parietal Lobe Dysfunction

  • Poor integration of touch
  • Proprioception issues
  • Spatial awareness challenges

Hyper/Hypo-Reactivity

  • Over-Responsive: Extreme reactions
  • Under-Responsive: Seeking intense input
  • Fight-or-flight responses

Subtypes

Sensory Modulation Disorder

  • Most common subtype
  • Difficulty regulating responses

Sensory-Based Motor Disorder

  • Clumsiness
  • Poor balance
  • Linked to cerebellum

Sensory Discrimination Disorder

  • Trouble distinguishing stimuli
  • Difficulty with hot vs. cold

Causes & Risk Factors

Genetic Factors

  • Family history of SPD
  • ADHD or ASD in family

Environmental Factors

  • Prematurity
  • Prenatal stress
  • Early childhood neglect

Treatment & Management

Occupational Therapy

  • Custom "sensory diets"
  • Weighted blankets
  • Swinging therapy

Adaptations

  • Noise-canceling headphones
  • Textured utensils
  • Environmental modifications

Emerging Research

  • Neuroplasticity in OT
  • Gut-Brain Axis studies
  • Probiotic research

Key Takeaways

  • SPD is a real neurological condition, not just behavioral
  • Early OT intervention improves daily functioning
  • 40–80% of autistic kids have SPD
  • 30–50% of ADHD kids have SPD

SPD Knowledge Quiz

1. Which brain lobe is most associated with SPD's sensory integration difficulties?

A) Frontal lobe
B) Parietal lobe
C) Occipital lobe
D) Temporal lobe
The parietal lobe is primarily responsible for sensory integration, making it the most associated with SPD's sensory processing difficulties.

2. True or False: SPD is officially listed as a standalone diagnosis in the DSM-5.

True
False
False. While SPD is recognized by occupational therapists, it is not currently listed as a standalone diagnosis in the DSM-5.

3. A child who craves spinning, crashing into furniture, or chewing on non-food items likely has:

A) Over-responsive SPD
B) Sensory discrimination disorder
C) Under-responsive SPD
D) Landau-Kleffner syndrome
These behaviors are characteristic of under-responsive SPD, where children seek out intense sensory input to meet their sensory needs.

4. Which therapy is most commonly used to treat SPD?

A) Speech therapy
B) Occupational therapy (OT)
C) Cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT)
D) Physical therapy
Occupational therapy is the primary treatment for SPD, using customized sensory diets and environmental adaptations to help manage symptoms.

5. Which of these is NOT a subtype of SPD?

A) Sensory modulation disorder
B) Sensory-based motor disorder
C) Auditory processing disorder
D) Sensory discrimination disorder
While auditory processing disorder can co-occur with SPD, it is not considered a subtype of SPD. The main subtypes are sensory modulation disorder, sensory-based motor disorder, and sensory discrimination disorder.

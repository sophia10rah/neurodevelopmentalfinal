Attention-Deficit/Hyperactivity Disorder (ADHD)

Key Facts & Research

Core Features of ADHD

Symptoms

  • Inattention
  • Hyperactivity
  • Impulsivity

Persistence

  • ~15% continue full symptoms in adulthood
  • ~50% remain impacted by symptoms

Associated Risks

  • Lower IQ
  • Socioeconomic challenges
  • Increased risk of substance use disorders

Brain Structure & Function

Structural Differences

  • Reduced cortical volume, surface area, and folding
  • Smaller anterior cingulate cortex (ACC)
  • Differences in basal ganglia, cerebellum, and corpus callosum

Medication Effects

  • Stimulants (e.g., Methylphenidate)
  • Normalize brain activation in attention regions

Causes of ADHD

Genetic Factors

  • 76% heritability
  • Dopamine system genes (DAT1, DRD4/DRD5)
  • Reduced dopamine activity in basal ganglia

Environmental Risks

  • Maternal smoking
  • Pesticide exposure
  • Maternal stress

Comorbidities

Oppositional Defiant Disorder (ODD)

  • 60% overlap
  • Shared brain structure differences

Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD)

  • 35â€“85% overlap
  • Shared genetic risk factors

Emerging Research: Gut-Brain Connection

Key Findings

  • Altered gut microbiome composition
  • Low Bifidobacterium linked to symptoms
  • Bidirectional effects via vagus nerve

Why ADHD Is Linked to the Frontal Lobe

Maturational Delay

  • Slower developmental trajectory of frontal lobes
  • Inconsistent cognitive and behavioral control
  • Reduced activation in right prefrontal cortex

Disrupted Brain Pathways

  • Direct pathway (facilitates actions)
  • Indirect pathway (inhibits impulses)
  • Hyperdirect pathway (rapidly stops responses)

Hemispheric Imbalance

  • Right hemisphere (attention control) underdeveloped
  • Left hemisphere (motor activity) overactive
  • Contributes to hyperactivity symptoms

Prefrontal-Basal Ganglia Circuits

  • PFC communicates with basal ganglia
  • Dysfunction leads to poor impulse control
  • Core ADHD symptoms emerge from circuit disruption

Leisman, G., & Melillo, R. (2022). Front and center: Maturational dysregulation of frontal lobe functional neuroanatomic connections in attention deficit hyperactivity disorder. Frontiers in neuroanatomy, 16, 936025. https://doi.org/10.3389/fnana.2022.936025

Summary

  • Brain structure differences in attention and impulse control regions
  • Combination of genetic and environmental causes
  • High comorbidity with ODD and ASD
  • Promising research in gut microbiome

ADHD Knowledge Quiz

1. Which brain region's reduced volume is linked to attention deficits in ADHD?

A) Hippocampus
B) Anterior cingulate cortex (ACC)
C) Occipital lobe
The anterior cingulate cortex (ACC) is notably smaller in individuals with ADHD and is directly linked to attention deficits.

2. True or False: Most children "outgrow" ADHD by adulthood.

True
False
False. While symptoms may change, about 50% of children with ADHD continue to experience symptoms into adulthood.

3. Which prenatal exposure is not linked to ADHD risk?

A) Pesticides
B) Maternal smoking
C) Thimerosal in vaccines
Thimerosal in vaccines is a debunked myth and has no scientific link to ADHD risk. Research has shown that vaccines are safe and do not cause ADHD.

4. Which neurotransmitter system is most directly implicated in ADHD symptoms?

A) Serotonin
B) Dopamine
C) GABA
D) Acetylcholine
ADHD is strongly linked to dopamine dysregulation, which affects focus and impulse control. Stimulant medications (e.g., methylphenidate) target dopamine pathways.

5. What percentage of children with ADHD also show symptoms of Oppositional Defiant Disorder (ODD)?

A) 10%
B) 35%
C) 60%
D) 85%
There is a 60% overlap between ADHD and ODD, with shared brain structure differences and behavioral challenges.

Landau-Kleffner Syndrome (LKS)

Key Facts & Research

Core Features of LKS

Definition

A rare childhood epileptic disorder causing sudden or gradual loss of language skills (auditory verbal agnosia).

Age of Onset

Typically 3–7 years old in previously normal-developing children.

Key Symptom

Can hear sounds but can't understand speech ("word deafness").

Brain Mechanisms

Temporal Lobe Seizures

  • Abnormal EEG spikes in bilateral temporal lobes during sleep
  • Disrupts Wernicke's area (language comprehension)

Silent Seizures

  • Often no obvious convulsions
  • Seizures occur mostly during sleep

Symptoms & Progression

Early Stage

  • Child stops responding to speech
  • May develop hyperactivity or aggression
  • Often misdiagnosed as hearing loss or autism

Later Stage

  • Expressive language deteriorates
  • May develop mutism or telegraphic speech
  • Often retains nonverbal skills

Causes & Risk Factors

Unknown Origin

  • No single cause identified
  • May involve GRIN2A gene mutations
  • Rarely linked to infections

Comorbidities

  • 80% develop clinical seizures
  • 20% only have EEG abnormalities
  • Often confused with autism

Diagnosis & Treatment

Diagnosis

  • Sleep EEG shows CSWS pattern
  • Must rule out autism and hearing loss
  • Requires specialized assessment

Treatments

  • Antiseizure medications
  • Corticosteroids/IVIG for immune cases
  • Speech therapy and alternative communication

Key Takeaways

  • LKS is not degenerative—some kids regain speech with treatment
  • Early intervention (before age 6) improves recovery
  • LKS is distinct from autism, though often confused
  • Critical window for treatment exists

LKS Knowledge Quiz

1. LKS primarily affects which brain lobe?

A) Frontal
B) Temporal
C) Parietal
D) Occipital
LKS primarily affects the temporal lobe, particularly the language processing areas.

2. True or False: LKS seizures always cause visible convulsions.

A) True
B) False
False. LKS seizures often occur during sleep and may not cause visible convulsions.

3. The hallmark symptom of LKS is:

A) Blindness
B) Muscle weakness
C) Sudden inability to understand speech
D) Memory loss
The hallmark symptom is auditory verbal agnosia - the sudden inability to understand speech despite normal hearing.

4. Which test is critical for diagnosing LKS?

A) MRI
B) Sleep EEG
C) Blood Test
D) Hearing test
Sleep EEG is critical as it shows the characteristic continuous spike-wave during slow-wave sleep pattern.

5. What percentage of LKS patients develop clinical seizures?

A) 20%
B) 50%
C) 80%
D) 100%
80% of LKS patients develop clinical seizures, while 20% only show EEG abnormalities.
